# Buyer Psychology & Personality Frameworks

## Big Five (OCEAN) Personality Model

The Big Five is the most empirically validated personality framework. Each trait exists on a spectrum from low to high.

### Openness to Experience (O)
- High scorers: Curious, innovative, risk-tolerant, drawn to novel ideas and change
- Low scorers: Conventional, prefer proven methods, resistant to change, risk-averse
- Buying behavior: High-O buyers respond to innovation, disruption narratives, and future-forward messaging. Low-O buyers want case studies, proven ROI, and step-by-step adoption paths.

### Conscientiousness (C)
- High scorers: Organized, detail-oriented, goal-driven, metrics-focused, thorough in evaluation
- Low scorers: Spontaneous, flexible, may act on gut instinct
- Buying behavior: High-C buyers need data, benchmarks, security guarantees, and structured demos. They are often the internal champions who build the business case. Low-C buyers can be moved faster with emotional appeals.

### Extraversion (E)
- High scorers: Outgoing, status-conscious, relationship-driven, enjoy recognition
- Low scorers: Introverted, prefer deep thinking, trust built over time, value written over verbal communication
- Buying behavior: High-E buyers respond to social proof, peer recognition, community, and high-energy pitches. Low-E buyers prefer thoughtful written content, whitepapers, and one-on-one conversations.

### Agreeableness (A)
- High scorers: Cooperative, empathetic, team-first, conflict-averse
- Low scorers: Competitive, direct, skeptical, challenge vendors
- Buying behavior: High-A buyers care about team adoption, ease of change management, and vendor relationships. Low-A buyers want to negotiate, challenge assumptions, and see competitive differentiators.

### Neuroticism (N)
- High scorers: Anxiety-prone, risk-sensitive, focused on potential downsides
- Low scorers: Calm, confident, emotionally stable, comfortable with uncertainty
- Buying behavior: High-N buyers need extensive risk mitigation: SLAs, exit clauses, security certifications, and references. Low-N buyers are comfortable moving faster and are less focused on edge cases.

## DISC Model

DISC categorizes behavior into four quadrants based on two axes: task vs. people focus, and active vs. reflective style.

### D — Dominance
- Profile: Direct, results-oriented, competitive, decisive, time-poor
- Values: Control, achievement, bottom-line results, speed
- Communication style: Skip the small talk. Get to ROI within 60 seconds. Use "you'll get X result" framing. Bullet points over paragraphs.
- Objections: "Prove the ROI", "What are competitors doing?", "How fast can I see results?"

### I — Influence
- Profile: Enthusiastic, optimistic, collaborative, motivated by recognition and relationships
- Values: Approval, social connection, innovation, excitement
- Communication style: Be warm and personal. Reference mutual connections. Tell stories. Use visionary language.
- Objections: "Will my team like this?", "Who else is doing it?", "Can we trial this together?"

### S — Steadiness
- Profile: Patient, loyal, methodical, team-player, dislikes conflict
- Values: Stability, harmony, predictability, process
- Communication style: Build trust first. Move slowly and deliberately. Emphasize support, onboarding, and change management.
- Objections: "What does implementation look like?", "Will this disrupt our current workflow?", "Who supports us post-sale?"

### C — Conscientiousness
- Profile: Analytical, quality-driven, detail-focused, systematic, skeptical
- Values: Accuracy, thoroughness, compliance, proven methods
- Communication style: Lead with data and evidence. Provide detailed specs. Answer every question. Avoid hyperbole.
- Objections: "Show me the methodology", "What are the limitations?", "Can I see the security audit?"

## Psychological Buying Triggers

### Loss Aversion
Prospects are more motivated by avoiding losses than gaining equivalent wins. Frame problems as risks the prospect is currently exposed to, not just opportunities they're missing.

### Social Proof
Humans calibrate behavior against peers. Citing specific companies similar to the prospect's industry and size is 3x more effective than generic testimonials.

### Authority Bias
Buyers defer to recognized experts. Third-party validation (analyst reports, industry awards, media coverage) increases perceived credibility.

### Reciprocity
Providing genuine value before asking for anything creates obligation. Free audits, personalized insights, and useful content before the pitch trigger reciprocity.

### Scarcity / Urgency
Time-limited offers or limited capacity drives action in D and I types. Less effective on C and S types who resist pressure.

### Cognitive Load Reduction
Complex decisions cause paralysis. Reduce choices to three options maximum. Use comparison tables. Make the "obvious choice" structure your recommendations.

## Personality Indicators from Professional Behavior

| Behavior Signal | Likely Trait |
|---|---|
| Frequent thought-leadership posts | High Openness, High Extraversion |
| Detailed job descriptions / metrics in bio | High Conscientiousness |
| Multiple committee roles / community involvement | High Agreeableness |
| Aggressive growth narratives in press releases | High Dominance (D) |
| Long tenure at company | High Steadiness (S) |
| Moves between companies frequently | High Openness, low Conscientiousness |
| Extensive technical certifications | High Conscientiousness (C-type) |
| Keynote speaker / conference presence | High Extraversion, High Influence (I) |

## Email Copywriting by Personality Type

### For High Conscientiousness / C-DISC
- Open with a data point or research finding
- Reference their specific industry metrics
- Provide a clear, logical argument
- Attach supporting evidence or case study
- CTA: Low pressure — "Would a 20-minute walkthrough make sense?"

### For High Dominance / D-DISC
- Subject line should imply a result: "How [Company] cut X by Y%"
- Keep body under 100 words
- Lead with the outcome, not the process
- Make the next step extremely easy
- CTA: Direct — "15 minutes Thursday at 2pm?"

### For High Influence / I-DISC
- Open with something personal or a shared connection
- Use warm, conversational tone
- Tell a story
- Emphasize the community / who else is doing this
- CTA: Collaborative — "Would love to get your take on this"

### For High Steadiness / S-DISC
- Open with empathy: acknowledge the challenge they face
- Emphasize stability and support throughout
- Provide a low-risk first step
- Highlight post-sale success stories
- CTA: Gentle — "Happy to send over more detail if useful"
